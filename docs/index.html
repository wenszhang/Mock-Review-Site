The Mahatma

Critics Consensus

In an age where movies expected to be no more than cascades of bright lights and superfluous violence, The Mahatma stands in grand defiance - proposing testion through sharing a genuine passion for the aspirations of the protagonist, Gandhi. Such bold contempt of modern cliches by the director is masterfully reflected in Gandhi’s equal recalcitrance to the malevolent state of his world. Having proven his profound leadership in South Africa, The Mahatma serves as a comprehensive sequel to Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi with grand exploration into profound ideas of revolution through peace - striving to prove that true progress, much like the greatest works of cinema, are not the results of an absurd obsession with bombs and guns.

The Mahatma centers around the life of Gandhi, of a lawyer turned political-ethicist, who recently arrived in the country of England amidst the beginnings of WWI. Having had found much time to write (courtesy of imprisonment in the name of peace), Gandhi returns home to India as a renowned hero. Rather than treat his status as a means of fame or fortune, Gandhi continues to spread his ideas of Satyagraha, soon founding a communal farm and soon-to-be base by which he and his followers would begin a march for independence.

Even before the march, Gandhi’s beliefs are continuously brought into question through brutal suppression by the British government. Though many believe in his teachings on non-violence, Gandhi is frequently challenged by his own people as well, who’s call for violent retaliation is only compounded through the slaughter of hundreds by Reginald Dyer on the grounds of the Golden Temple. Despite the breadth such discourse, Gandhi stands tall in the face of violence - even if such could bring his demise.
